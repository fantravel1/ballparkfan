Nationals Park

Baseball in the nation's capital. The Capitol dome rises beyond the outfield, giant racing presidents sprint around the warning track, and the Anacostia River waterfront stretches out beyond the gates of the park where the 2019 World Series champions play.

What Makes Nationals Park Special

Nationals Park occupies a unique position in Major League Baseball. It is the only ballpark in the nation's capital, situated along the Anacostia River in what was once an underused industrial waterfront. When it opened in 2008, it was a catalyst for an entire neighborhood's transformation. More than that, it is a ballpark where the symbols of American government are literally part of the backdrop, where the game unfolds with the Capitol dome on the horizon and the weight of Washington, DC all around you.

The Capitol Dome on the Skyline

From the upper deck on the third-base side, the illuminated dome of the United States Capitol rises above the outfield against the night sky. It is a view that exists nowhere else in professional sports. There is something powerful about watching a baseball game with the seat of American democracy framed in the distance, especially during evening games when the dome glows white against the darkening sky. The Capitol view gives Nationals Park a sense of place that is impossible to replicate. Other parks have city skylines and mountain ranges; this one has the building where the laws of the country are made.

The Navy Yard Waterfront

Nationals Park sits at the heart of the Navy Yard neighborhood, directly along the Anacostia River. The waterfront boardwalk and Yards Park are steps from the stadium gates, giving the ballpark an urban waterfront setting that has matured beautifully since the park opened. Before and after games, fans walk along the river, eat at waterfront restaurants, and enjoy an atmosphere that blends the energy of a game-day crowd with the relaxed vibe of a riverfront neighborhood. The Navy Yard has gone from a forgotten corner of DC to one of its most vibrant districts, and the ballpark is the anchor of that transformation.

The Presidents Race

Every home game during the middle of the fourth inning, giant foam-headed mascots of George Washington, Thomas Jefferson, Abraham Lincoln, Theodore Roosevelt, and William Howard Taft race around the warning track from center field to home plate. The Presidents Race is one of the most entertaining between-innings traditions in all of baseball. For years, Teddy Roosevelt famously never won, suffering an epic 525-race losing streak that became a running saga across the sport. When Teddy finally won in 2012, it was national news. The races feature scripted comedy, surprise cameos, and elaborate sabotage scenarios that keep fans entertained and laughing regardless of the score. It is the signature moment of every Nationals home game.

2019 World Series Champions

The Washington Nationals won the 2019 World Series in one of the most improbable championship runs in baseball history. After starting the season 19-31, the Nationals clawed their way back, survived a wild-card game thriller against the Brewers, beat the Dodgers and the Cardinals in the NLDS and NLCS, and then defeated the Houston Astros in seven games — winning all four games on the road in Houston. The "Stay in the Fight" rally cry, the Baby Shark anthem, and the image of the team celebrating on the field in Houston are etched into Washington sports history. The 2019 championship gave DC its first World Series title since 1924 and transformed Nationals Park from a nice ballpark into a place with genuine postseason ghosts.

Cherry Blossom Season Games

Washington, DC is world-famous for its cherry blossoms, and the Nationals' early-season home games coincide with peak bloom in late March and early April. Attending a game during cherry blossom season means combining one of America's most beautiful natural events with a day at the ballpark. The Tidal Basin is blanketed in pink, the city is alive with visitors, and the weather is crisp and perfect for baseball. The Nationals lean into it with cherry blossom-themed jerseys, merchandise, and promotions. There is no better time to visit Nationals Park than during peak bloom.

LEED-Certified Green Design

Nationals Park was the first major professional sports venue in the United States to receive LEED Silver certification for environmental design. The park incorporates sustainable features including a green roof, energy-efficient lighting, water conservation systems, and recycled building materials. It was a pioneering commitment to sustainability in professional sports, and while many venues have followed since, Nationals Park was the one that proved it could be done at scale. The green design is not something most fans will notice on a casual visit, but it is woven into the bones of the building.

Local Travel

Washington, DC is one of the great cities of the world, and a trip to Nationals Park is an excuse to experience the extraordinary collection of monuments, museums, and neighborhoods that make the nation's capital unlike any other American city. Nearly everything listed here is free.

National Mall & Monuments

2 miles

The National Mall is the symbolic heart of America, stretching two miles from the Lincoln Memorial to the United States Capitol. Along the way you will pass the Washington Monument, the World War II Memorial, the Vietnam Veterans Memorial, the Korean War Veterans Memorial, the Martin Luther King Jr. Memorial, and the Reflecting Pool. The Lincoln Memorial alone — standing inside the temple, reading the words of the Gettysburg Address carved into the walls, looking out over the Reflecting Pool toward the Washington Monument — is one of the most moving experiences you can have in this country. All of it is free, open to the public, and accessible by Metro. The Smithsonian museums line both sides of the Mall and are also entirely free. You could spend a week here and not see everything.

The Wharf

1 mile

The Wharf is DC's newest and most ambitious waterfront development, a mile-long stretch along the Washington Channel packed with restaurants, bars, live music venues, and a revitalized fish market that has been operating in some form since 1805. The dining options range from upscale seafood to casual waterfront beer gardens, and the energy on a warm evening is electric. Water taxis connect The Wharf to Georgetown and Alexandria, Virginia, making it easy to explore multiple waterfronts in a single day. On game days, The Wharf is a natural pre-game destination — close enough to Nationals Park to walk, lively enough to spend an entire afternoon before first pitch.

Capitol Hill & Library of Congress

1.5 miles

Capitol Hill is the neighborhood surrounding the United States Capitol building and is home to some of the most important institutions in the country. Free guided tours of the Capitol are available and take you through the Rotunda, National Statuary Hall, and the Crypt. The Library of Congress — the largest library in the world — is a breathtaking building that is worth visiting for the architecture alone, with its ornate Great Hall and stunning Main Reading Room. The Supreme Court building is across the street and open to visitors when the Court is not in session. A few blocks away, Eastern Market is a beloved weekend destination with local vendors, fresh produce, artisan goods, and an outdoor flea market that captures the community character of Capitol Hill.

Smithsonian Museums

2 miles

The Smithsonian Institution operates 21 museums in Washington, DC, and every single one of them is free. The National Air and Space Museum houses the Wright Flyer, the Spirit of St. Louis, and a lunar module. The National Museum of Natural History has the Hope Diamond and a hall of dinosaur fossils. The National Museum of American History holds the original Star-Spangled Banner, Dorothy's ruby slippers, and Abraham Lincoln's top hat. The National Museum of African American History and Culture, opened in 2016, is a masterpiece that traces the African American experience from slavery to the present. These are not regional museums — they are among the finest in the world, and Washington gives them away for nothing. Plan to visit at least two or three during your trip.

Navy Yard Boardwalk

Adjacent

The Navy Yard boardwalk runs directly along the Anacostia River, just steps from Nationals Park, and has become one of DC's most popular outdoor gathering spots. Yards Park anchors the boardwalk with a terraced lawn, a wading canal, a pedestrian bridge, and riverfront seating areas that fill up on warm evenings. Waterfront restaurants and bars line the boardwalk, and the views of the Anacostia River — especially at sunset — are surprisingly beautiful for a neighborhood that was largely industrial a generation ago. On game days, the boardwalk buzzes with pre-game energy. On non-game days, it is a peaceful riverside walk that showcases how dramatically this corner of Washington has been transformed.

Getting There

Metro

The Navy Yard-Ballpark station on the Green Line is the primary transit option for reaching Nationals Park, and it is excellent. The station is two blocks from the center field gate, making it one of the shortest walks from public transit to a ballpark in all of Major League Baseball. The Green Line connects to the rest of the Metro system at L'Enfant Plaza, Gallery Place-Chinatown, and other transfer stations, giving you access from virtually anywhere in the DC, Maryland, and Virginia metropolitan area. On game days, Metro runs additional service, though the station can get extremely crowded in the 15 minutes immediately following the final out. If you can wait a bit after the game, the crush clears quickly.

DC Circulator & Water Taxi

The DC Circulator bus system offers an affordable way to get around the central areas of the city, and several routes pass within walking distance of the Navy Yard. For a more scenic approach, water taxis operated by the Potomac Riverboat Company connect The Wharf, Georgetown, and Alexandria to the Navy Yard waterfront, making it possible to arrive at the ballpark by boat. It is one of the more memorable ways to approach a baseball game anywhere in the country.

Bike Share & Parking

Capital Bikeshare stations are located near the stadium, and the Navy Yard neighborhood is well-connected by bike lanes that make cycling a practical and enjoyable option. If you are driving, several parking garages serve the Navy Yard area, but expect to pay premium game-day rates — typically $25 to $40 — and plan to arrive early, as garage capacity fills quickly for popular games. Street parking in the neighborhood is extremely limited on game days. Metro is the strongly recommended option.

Fan Tips

Insider Tips

Stay in your seat for the Presidents Race during the middle of the fourth inning. It is the signature entertainment at Nationals Park, and even veteran fans who have seen it dozens of times still watch to see what comedy or sabotage will unfold. First-time visitors are often surprised at how genuinely entertaining and elaborate the race is. It is not just a mascot sprint — it is a production, and it is worth your full attention.

Ben's Chili Bowl, the legendary DC institution, has a location inside Nationals Park serving their famous half-smokes — a spicy pork and beef sausage smothered in chili, mustard, and onions. It is a DC culinary landmark that has been on U Street since 1958, and having one at the ballpark is a way to taste a piece of Washington history without leaving the stadium. Get the half-smoke with chili. Do not overthink it.

If you can plan your visit during cherry blossom season in late March or early April, do it. The combination of peak bloom on the Tidal Basin, the Nationals' early-season home stand, and the crisp spring weather creates one of the most magical baseball experiences in the country. Walk the blossoms in the morning, visit a museum in the afternoon, and head to the ballpark in the evening. It is a perfect Washington day.

Honest Drawbacks

Worth Knowing

The Navy Yard neighborhood, while improving rapidly, is still developing. Outside of the immediate waterfront blocks, the area can feel sparse, with construction sites, empty lots, and stretches that lack the walkable character of more established DC neighborhoods like Georgetown, Dupont Circle, or Adams Morgan. The transformation is real and ongoing, but it is not yet a neighborhood where you can wander for hours and find something interesting on every block.

The stadium design itself is somewhat bland. Nationals Park is a perfectly functional modern ballpark, but it lacks the architectural personality and distinctive character of the best stadiums in the league. The exterior is unremarkable, and the interior, while clean and comfortable, does not have the wow factor of a PNC Park, Oracle Park, or Camden Yards. It is a good park, not a great one, in terms of pure design.

Washington Nationals fandom is still growing. DC was without baseball for 33 years between the Senators' departure in 1971 and the Nationals' arrival in 2005, and the city's fan culture has not yet reached the depth and intensity of longer-established baseball cities. Attendance can be thin for midweek games against less popular opponents, and the stadium atmosphere on those nights can feel flat. When the Nationals are competitive and the park is full, the energy is fantastic — but consistency is still a work in progress.

Washington, DC summers are hot and humid. July and August games can be oppressive, with temperatures in the 90s and humidity levels that make the air feel heavy and sticky. The open-air design of Nationals Park offers limited shade in most seating areas during day games, and even evening games can be uncomfortably warm. Hydrate aggressively, wear sunscreen, and consider evening games if you are visiting during the peak of summer.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you see the Capitol from Nationals Park?

Yes, you can see the U.S. Capitol dome from Nationals Park, and it is one of the most distinctive visual features of the stadium. The Capitol is visible from the upper deck seating areas along the third-base side and from portions of the main concourse. The view is particularly striking during evening games when the dome is illuminated against the night sky, creating a backdrop that no other ballpark in America can match. The sight of the Capitol rising beyond the outfield is a constant reminder that you are watching baseball in the nation's capital, and it gives Nationals Park a sense of place and gravity that elevates the entire experience. It is not visible from every seat, so if the view matters to you, aim for upper-level sections on the third-base side.

What is the Presidents Race at Nationals Park?

The Presidents Race is Nationals Park's signature between-innings entertainment, held during the middle of the fourth inning at every home game. Giant foam-headed mascots representing George Washington, Thomas Jefferson, Abraham Lincoln, and Theodore Roosevelt race around the warning track from center field to the home plate area. A fifth president, William Howard Taft, was added to the race in 2013. The race has become one of the most beloved traditions in all of baseball, famous for its scripted comedy, surprise guest appearances, and elaborate sabotage scenarios. For years, Teddy Roosevelt was the lovable loser who never won a race, and his eventual victory in 2012 after a 525-race losing streak was a genuine cultural moment in Washington, DC. The Presidents Race is a must-see part of any Nationals Park experience.

How do you get to Nationals Park by Metro?

Nationals Park is easily accessible via the Washington Metro system. The Navy Yard-Ballpark station on the Green Line is located just two blocks from the stadium's center field gate, making it the most convenient option for reaching the park without a car. On game days, Metro runs additional service and the station can get crowded immediately after the final out, so consider waiting 15 to 20 minutes after the game ends to avoid the worst of the rush. The Green Line connects to the rest of the Metro system at L'Enfant Plaza, Gallery Place-Chinatown, and other transfer stations, giving you access from virtually anywhere in the DC metropolitan area. Metro is by far the recommended way to get to Nationals Park, as parking in the Navy Yard area is limited and expensive.
