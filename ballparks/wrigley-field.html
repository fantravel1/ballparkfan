What Makes Wrigley Field Special

Wrigley Field is not just a ballpark. It is a living artifact of baseball history, wedged into a residential neighborhood on Chicago's North Side, surrounded by apartment buildings, corner bars, and the electric energy of Wrigleyville. Opened in 1914 and home to the Cubs since 1916, it is the second-oldest active ballpark in Major League Baseball and one of the most cherished stadiums in all of professional sports.

The Ivy-Covered Outfield Walls

Planted in 1937 by Bill Veeck, the ivy that covers Wrigley's outfield walls is the park's most iconic feature. By late summer the walls are a lush green curtain of Boston ivy and Japanese bittersweet. A ball lost in the ivy is ruled a ground-rule double, a rule unique to this park. There is nothing else like it in professional baseball.

The Hand-Operated Scoreboard

Rising above the center field bleachers, the scoreboard at Wrigley has been manually operated since 1937. Workers inside the board change the inning-by-inning scores of every Major League game by hand using metal number plates. It remains one of the last hand-turned scoreboards in baseball, a stubborn and beautiful holdout against the digital age.

Rooftop Seating Across Waveland and Sheffield

The buildings across Waveland Avenue and Sheffield Avenue beyond the outfield walls have been converted into rooftop seating areas, offering a uniquely Chicago way to watch a game. These rooftop clubs serve food and drinks with a view directly into the ballpark. Though some sightlines were affected by the 2014-2019 renovation and new video boards, the rooftops remain an unmissable part of the Wrigley experience.

Day Baseball Tradition

Wrigley Field was the last Major League park to install permanent lights, holding out until 1988. The tradition of day baseball is still alive: the Cubs play more day games than any other team, and there is nothing quite like a sunny afternoon at Wrigley with a cold beer and a Chicago dog. Afternoon games at Wrigley have a rhythm and atmosphere that night games at other parks simply cannot replicate.

The Friendly Confines

Ernie Banks coined the nickname, and it stuck. Wrigley's intimate size, its proximity to the street, and the way the neighborhood wraps around the park on all sides give it a warmth and accessibility that modern stadiums cannot reproduce. You can hear the crowd from blocks away. You can smell the hot dogs from the L platform. The park feels like it belongs to the city in a way few venues do.

The Marquee Sign

The red Wrigley Field marquee at the corner of Clark and Addison is one of the most photographed landmarks in Chicago. It has welcomed fans since 1934 and serves as the unofficial meeting point before every game. Whether it reads "Home of Chicago Cubs" or announces a concert, the marquee is the front door to one of baseball's great cathedrals.

Local Travel & Nearby Attractions

Chicago is one of the best baseball trip cities in America. Wrigley's neighborhood alone could fill a weekend, but the rest of the city is worth every extra day you can spare.

Wrigleyville Neighborhood

Steps away

The blocks surrounding Wrigley Field are the best ballpark neighborhood in baseball. Murphy's Bleachers is the legendary pre-game bar directly across from the bleacher entrance. Sluggers has batting cages and a dueling piano bar upstairs. Clark Street is lined with sports bars, restaurants, and souvenir shops. After a win, the neighborhood erupts. After a loss, it consoles you. Either way, you will not want to leave.

Millennium Park & The Bean

5 miles south

Chicago's crown jewel public space is home to Cloud Gate (the Bean), the Jay Pritzker Pavilion with its stunning Frank Gehry-designed bandshell, and the Crown Fountain with its interactive video towers. Free concerts, public art, and gorgeous lakefront views make this a must-visit on any non-game day. Take the Red Line south to Washington/State.

Chicago Architecture Center River Cruise

6 miles south

Widely considered one of the best boat tours in the world, the Chicago Architecture Center River Cruise takes you along the Chicago River past over 50 landmark buildings with expert docent narration. You will learn about the city's rebuilding after the Great Fire and the architects who shaped the modern skyline. Book in advance during summer months.

Lincoln Park Zoo

2 miles south

One of the last free-admission zoos in the country, Lincoln Park Zoo sits on 35 acres along the lakefront. It is an easy walk or short bus ride from Wrigleyville and makes a perfect morning activity before an afternoon game. The adjacent Lincoln Park Conservatory and North Pond are also worth exploring.

Navy Pier

5 miles southeast

Chicago's lakefront landmark features the Centennial Wheel (a 196-foot Ferris wheel), restaurants, museums, boat tours, and fireworks on summer evenings. It is touristy, but the views of the skyline from the pier at sunset are hard to beat. Take the CTA bus #29 from the Wrigleyville area or ride the Red Line south and transfer.

Getting There

Wrigley Field is one of the most transit-accessible ballparks in Major League Baseball. The CTA Red Line Addison station is steps from the main entrance at Clark and Addison. Step off the train, walk half a block, and you are at the ballpark. It is that simple.

Several CTA bus routes also serve the area, including the #152 Addison, #22 Clark, and #8 Halsted lines. All drop off within a short walk of the gates.

If you are driving, know this: parking in Wrigleyville is scarce and expensive on game days. Street parking is restricted to permit holders in the surrounding residential blocks. Private lots and garages exist but fill up fast and charge premium prices. The overwhelming recommendation for Wrigley Field is to take the L. It is faster, cheaper, and part of the experience.

Fan Tips

Insider Tips

Sit in the bleachers for the best atmosphere. The bleacher section at Wrigley is its own universe: louder, rowdier, and more social than the rest of the park. It is the section where traditions live.

Grab an Old Style beer. It is the unofficial beer of Wrigley Field and has been for decades. It is cheap, it is cold, and it tastes better here than anywhere else on Earth. Trust the tradition.

Do not miss the seventh-inning stretch. Wrigley's version is famous: a guest conductor leads the crowd in "Take Me Out to the Ball Game" from the broadcast booth, a tradition started by Harry Caray. Stand up, sing loud, and do not be shy.

Arrive early and walk Clark Street. The pre-game scene in Wrigleyville is half the experience. Grab food, duck into a bar, soak in the energy. The neighborhood comes alive hours before first pitch.

Honest Drawbacks

Worth Knowing

Parking is essentially nonexistent. If you drive, you will pay a premium for a private lot and still walk several blocks. The CTA is the only sane option.

The concourses are cramped. Wrigley was built in 1914 and it shows in the narrow walkways and tight corridors behind the seating bowl. Getting food or finding a restroom during a packed game requires patience.

Some seats have obstructed views. Support columns and structural beams from the original construction can partially block sightlines in certain sections of the upper deck and behind home plate. Check your seat map before buying.

The neighborhood gets very crowded on game days. Wrigleyville is a party, and that is part of its charm, but it also means packed sidewalks, long bar waits, and noise levels that are not for everyone. Plan accordingly if you are traveling with small children or prefer a quieter experience.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Wrigley Field the oldest MLB stadium?

Wrigley Field is the second-oldest MLB stadium still in active use. It opened on April 23, 1914, two years after Fenway Park in Boston (1912). Originally called Weeghman Park, it was built for the Chicago Whales of the Federal League before the Cubs moved in for the 1916 season. Despite extensive renovations over the decades, Wrigley retains its original steel-and-concrete structure and intimate neighborhood feel.

What should I eat at Wrigley Field?

Inside the park, the Chicago-style hot dog (no ketchup) is the classic choice. You can also find Italian beef sandwiches, deep-dish pizza slices, and Giordano's stuffed pizza at select concession stands. Outside the park, Wrigleyville is packed with options: Murphy's Bleachers is the iconic pre-game spot, and Clark Street has dozens of bars and restaurants within walking distance. For a true Chicago experience, pair your hot dog with an Old Style beer.

Can you sit on the rooftops at Wrigley Field?

Yes, several buildings on Waveland and Sheffield Avenues beyond the outfield walls offer rooftop seating with views into Wrigley Field. These rooftop venues are privately operated and typically offer all-inclusive ticket packages that include food and drinks. Since the 2014-2019 renovations added large video boards and outfield structures, some rooftop sightlines have been partially obstructed, but they remain a unique and popular way to experience a Cubs game. Availability varies and advance booking is recommended.
