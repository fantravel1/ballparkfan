Common Questions

Ballpark Travel FAQ

What is the oldest MLB ballpark still in use?

Fenway Park in Boston, home of the Red Sox, opened on April 20, 1912, making it the oldest active Major League Baseball stadium. Wrigley Field in Chicago (1914) is second oldest. Both parks have been extensively renovated but retain their original character and charm.

Which MLB ballpark has the best food?

Oracle Park in San Francisco consistently ranks at the top for food with its garlic fries, Dungeness crab sandwiches, and Ghirardelli sundaes. T-Mobile Park in Seattle (garlic fries, sushi), Petco Park in San Diego (local craft beer, fish tacos), and PNC Park in Pittsburgh (Primanti Brothers sandwiches) are all elite food parks. The best ballpark food usually reflects the city's local food culture.

Which MLB ballpark is the easiest to reach by public transit?

Wrigley Field (CTA Red Line), Yankee Stadium (NYC Subway 4/B/D trains), Oracle Park (Muni Metro N line), and Fenway Park (MBTA Green Line) are all directly served by subway or light rail stops within a short walk of the gates. Camden Yards in Baltimore is also well-served by the Light Rail.

What is the largest MLB ballpark by seating capacity?

Dodger Stadium in Los Angeles is the largest MLB ballpark by seating capacity, holding approximately 56,000 fans. It has been the Dodgers' home since 1962 and sits on 300 acres in Chavez Ravine with panoramic views of the San Gabriel Mountains and downtown LA skyline.

How many days do you need for an MLB ballpark road trip?

A focused regional trip hitting 5-7 ballparks works well in 7-10 days. The classic Northeast corridor (Fenway, Yankee Stadium, Citi Field, Citizens Bank Park, Camden Yards) can be done in a week. A full 30-park marathon typically requires 30-45 days depending on scheduling and travel pace. Each ballpark deserves at least a day to explore the surrounding neighborhood and local food.
